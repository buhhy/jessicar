Inkling Library for Work and Leisure

I worked on Inkling's Product Design team as a User Experience Design Intern from September to December 2013. Although my responsibilities included assisting product designers with their projects or to create assets for them, I also led my own projects. One of these projects was the paginated library. It all began with a brief meeting with my manager, who said "I have a project for you. I don't know the details, but you should find out more from George."

I could barely prepare any questions for George beforehand. When we met, I understood him as our product specialist, in that he knew our users very well. He frequently talked to and worked with our users.

From our conversation, I recorded the user feedback he has heard for the library. I learned that our users would wait over a minute to load the page, then use cmd+f to search the page for their project. The library also sometimes had two versions with the same project names, but were distinguishable by examining their URLs. I condensed these concerns into two main problems:

Slow Loading Times

Many users who were publishing professionals had to wait over a minute to load the page before they could start working.

Difficult to Find Projects

Publishing professionals who had hundreds, or even thousands, of projects had great difficulty finding a particular project or version.

Old Inkling Library

There was another challenge to consider when designing the new library. A good number of users who were publishing professionals also used Inkling to read for leisure. The new design has to include the ability to switch or toggle between work projects and personal reading.
After wireframing ideas, I came up with two hypotheses to address the slow loading times and difficulty with finding projects:

Pagination

will significantly reduce loading times in the library. Long loading times are costly to the user and to the company if people need to wait before they begin work. The solution is to introduce pagination to the library, so that not all of the books are loaded at once.

Smarter sorting and search

allows users to find books faster. Scrolling through hundreds of books to find a specific one is very inefficient. Users searched for their books with the browser's "Find on page" feature (ctrl+F or cmd+F) after the page loaded.

After many iterations, the following design solution was approved by the project manager and sent for development.

Publishing Professionals

Publishing professionals use Inkling for both personal and work uses. Therefore, the solution allows them to easily tab between their uses.
